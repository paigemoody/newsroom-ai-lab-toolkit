# 🖼️ Framework: Design Thinking for Newsrooms

### Starting a new reporting project doesn’t mean you already know the headline \- or even the angle. 

You might spot something intriguing, a gap, or a shift. You get curious. You talk to people. You gather observations, facts, and perspectives before determining what the story really is. Your job is to surface insights, not assume you already know the lede.

This is essentially the mindset at the core of **‘Design Thinking’** \-  a methodology that begins with an **empathetic understanding of what’s happening for real people** (in this case, you, your colleagues and possibly folks you serve) setting aside assumptions about possible solutions. 

Just like in a reporting project, we starting with clues, hunches, and observations to **get to the root of what’s happening currently, and then let the process shape where we go**.

## Design Thinking \- Quick Overview

Design thinking is an ✨ iterative process ✨ in which we seek to:

- **understand the experiences of real people,**   
- **challenge assumptions,**   
- **redefine problems** and   
- **create innovative solutions which you can quickly prototype and test** 

The goal is to **identify alternative strategies and solutions that are not instantly apparent** with our initial level of understanding. 

### A Quick Origin Story

🌱 **Roots in the 1960s–70s:** Design Thinking emerged as designers and engineers formalized creative problem-solving methods. Emphasis on rapid prototyping and testing through observation \- *design as a scientific approach to problem solving.*

🧪 **Practical evolution \- 2004 →  :** Refined by practitioners at Stanford’s d.school and IDEO, emphasizing empathy and experimentation.

🌍 **Broader adoption:** By the 1990s–2000s, it spread beyond design—into business, education, and social innovation—as a human-centered framework for tackling complex challenges.

### Why we’re not jumping to solutions (yet)

Leaping right into solutions often means limiting ourselves to our current assumptions and what we already know how to do — which means we miss out on real clarity that can lead to more effective and creative outcomes.

In journalism terms, this stage in the process is similar to gathering possible reporting leads and tips – some might pan out, others won’t, but we don’t want to rule any out too early on in the process. **Some of the most compelling stories come from reporting that leads us somewhere we couldn’t have predicted at the start**.
